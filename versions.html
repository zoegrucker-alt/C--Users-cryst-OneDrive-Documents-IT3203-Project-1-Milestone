HTTP/1.0 and 1.1

By 1996, HTTP/1.0 introduced headers and status codes, giving machines a richer vocabulary. Soon after, HTTP/1.1 followed in 1997 — providing persistent connections, caching, and efficiency that allowed the web to scale globally.

HTTP/2 and 3

Standardized in 2015, HTTP/2 brought multiplexing and binary framing, reducing delays and speeding up content delivery. HTTP/3, built on Google’s QUIC protocol, shifted to UDP to improve connection stability and reduce latency.

Looking Ahead

The future of HTTP promises even greater speed, privacy, and adaptability. From the Internet of Things to blockchain and 5G, HTTP will continue to evolve — remaining the invisible foundation of our connected world.
